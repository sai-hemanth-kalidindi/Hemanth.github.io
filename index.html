Deadly Streets: A Spatial and Behavioral Analysis of Traffic Fatalities in New York City

Jan 2024 – May 2024

Explored spatial and temporal trends in fatal crashes across NYC neighborhoods.

Used Vision Zero data to analyze crash causes, victim profiles, and high-risk zones.

Applied regression and chi-square tests to examine behavioral and policy-related factors.

Visualized fatality clusters with heatmaps and borough-level breakdowns.

Objective
This project explores the critical public safety question:
Do neighborhoods with higher traffic-related fatalities share specific temporal, spatial, or behavioral risk factors that contribute to elevated fatality rates in NYC?
Using over two million crash records from the NYC Vision Zero program, the project investigates how time of day, alcohol involvement, borough location, and road user types influence fatal crash outcomes. The goal is to uncover hidden patterns that can inform more equitable, data-driven traffic safety policies targeting the city’s most vulnerable populations and high-risk areas.

Data Sources
The primary dataset for this project was sourced from NYC Open Data, specifically the Vision Zero: Motor Vehicle Collisions - Crashes dataset. This dataset includes over two million records of crash incidents reported across New York City's five boroughs.

Source: NYC Open Data – Motor Vehicle Collisions (Crashes)

Data Fields: Crash date/time, location, number of persons killed/injured, contributing factors (e.g., alcohol, speeding), borough, and road user type (pedestrian, cyclist, motorist).

Time Range: Multi-year span (2012–2025)

Data Format: CSV
Accessed and Processed in: Google Colab

Methods & Code Snippets
To explore the patterns behind fatal traffic crashes in NYC, I used a multi-method statistical approach. Each method was implemented in Python using libraries like pandas, matplotlib, seaborn, and statsmodels, with the full analysis conducted in Google Colab.
🔍 Analytical Methods
1.
Independent Sample T-Test
Compared the mean number of fatalities during nighttime (12 AM–6 AM) vs. daytime (6 AM–11:59 PM) crashes to determine if late-night crashes are more deadly.
Result: Fatal crashes are significantly more likely at night (p < 0.00001).

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Regression
Assessed how variables like alcohol involvement, crash time, and borough interact to influence the number of fatalities.
Included interaction terms such as NIGHT × ALCOHOL and NIGHT × BOROUGH.

Chi-Square Test of Independence
Evaluated whether risky driver behaviors (e.g., alcohol use, failure to yield) were statistically associated with fatal crashes.

🔗 View processed dataset and code on GitHub


Key Insights & Results
Through statistical analysis and geospatial visualization, this project revealed critical insights into the factors contributing to traffic fatalities in New York City:
📊 Key Findings

Time of Day Matters: Fatal crashes are significantly more likely to occur between 12 AM and 6 AM, driven by low visibility, driver fatigue, and increased impaired driving (T-test p-value < 0.00001).
Alcohol Use is Deadly: Crashes involving alcohol are strongly associated with higher fatality rates. This was validated using both OLS regression and Chi-Square tests.


Spatial Disparities:
Brooklyn and Queens report the highest number of fatalities overall.
Midtown Manhattan and Downtown Brooklyn are high-risk zones for fatal crashes, as shown in heatmaps and cluster maps.

Vulnerable Road Users: Pedestrians make up over 50% of all traffic fatalities, highlighting the urgent need for improved crosswalks, signage, and public awareness.

Seasonal Trends: Fatalities peak during summer months, especially August, and drop during colder months. This suggests a need for seasonal enforcement strategies.

Speed, Safety, and Circuits – Formula 1 Crash Analysis (1950–2020)

Feb 2024 – Apr 2024

This project analyzes traffic fatality patterns in NYC to identify behavioral and spatial risk factors.

Title and Objective
How have crash patterns in Formula 1 changed over time, and what role have safety innovations played in reducing fatal incidents?
This project analyzes 70 years of F1 crash data to evaluate the relationship between vehicle improvements, regulation changes, and race-related fatalities.

Data Sources
• Source: Ergast API, FIA historic archives, and Kaggle (F1 Crashes Dataset)
• Format: CSV and JSON
• Key Fields: Year, Driver, Crash Type, Fatality, Circuit, Safety Innovations
GitHub: [Your GitHub Link Here]

Methods & Code Snippets
• Cleaned datasets for driver retirements due to crashes.
• Time-series plots showed crash trends by decade.
• Categorical analysis by track, weather, and vehicle manufacturer.
• Regression models evaluated correlation between regulation years (like HALO in 2018) and fatal crash reduction.

Example:
f1_data['crash_flag'] = f1_data['status'].str.contains('accident').astype(int)
crash_trend = f1_data.groupby('year')['crash_flag'].mean()
plt.plot(crash_trend)

Key Insights & Results
• Fatal crash rates have significantly decreased since the 1990s due to modern track design and driver safety gear.
• The HALO system introduced in 2018 is correlated with a sharp drop in severe head injuries.
• Historic circuits like Nürburgring and Spa-Francorchamps had higher fatality rates due to outdated infrastructure.

Reflections
It was challenging to normalize data across decades due to format inconsistencies. However, visualizing the long-term safety evolution in Formula 1 was incredibly rewarding.
In future iterations, integrating car telemetry or tire degradation data could enhance the predictive understanding of crash causes.

Air Turbulence and Flight Safety

Mar 2024 – May 2024

This project analyzes traffic fatality patterns in NYC to identify behavioral and spatial risk factors.

Title and Objective
Turbulence in the skies remains one of the leading causes of in-flight injuries and flight disruptions. This project explores: 
How does air turbulence vary by flight route, time, and atmospheric conditions, and what patterns can help improve aviation safety?
The objective is to analyze publicly available aviation incident data to identify trends in turbulence-related incidents, 
pinpoint high-risk airspaces, and propose predictive measures that can enhance airline safety responses.

Data Sources
• Source: NOAA’s Aviation Weather Center and NTSB Aviation Accident Database
• Format: CSV, historical incident logs
• Fields Used: Flight date, location, aircraft type, turbulence severity, injuries reported, atmospheric data
• Tools: Python, Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn
GitHub: [Your GitHub Link Here]

Methods & Code Snippets
• Cleaned and merged turbulence incident logs with weather data.
• Used time-based analysis to examine turbulence frequency by hour and month.
• Mapped hotspots using latitude/longitude fields and basemap plotting.
• Developed a simple classification model to predict turbulence risk by route and weather severity.

Example:
df['month'] = pd.to_datetime(df['incident_date']).dt.month
df['is_severe'] = df['turbulence_level'].apply(lambda x: 1 if x == 'Severe' else 0)
sns.countplot(x='month', hue='is_severe', data=df)

Key Insights & Results
• Turbulence is more frequent during afternoon and evening flights.
• Peak incidents occur during spring and early summer, aligning with jet stream activity.
• Routes over mountainous regions (e.g., Rockies, Andes) had the highest severe turbulence reports.
• Heatmaps identified turbulence clusters near common flight corridors, providing insight for re-routing policies.

Reflections
This project required overcoming challenges with merging geospatial weather data and ensuring accurate time zone alignment across flights. 
It was rewarding to create visual maps that could have real-world safety implications. In the future, I would expand the model to include real-time wind shear and altitude patterns for better risk forecasting.
